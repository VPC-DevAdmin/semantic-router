Executive summary

Most production traffic to a large language model is easy. A user asks for general info, a quick rewrite, a common business question, or a bug fix. A small fraction is hard: a multi-step proof, or a synthesis that spans several domains. When every request is sent to a single frontier model, the easy questions are answered at the same price as the hard ones. That price gap is the waste a semantic router removes.

A semantic router reads each incoming prompt, estimates how much capability it needs, and forwards it to the smallest model that can answer it well. Trivial questions go to a small, inexpensive model, and the few that genuinely need a frontier model still get one. Answer quality stays the same on a per-question basis while the blended cost falls sharply.

This paper documents a working five-tier router built on the open-source vLLM Semantic Router, run as an on-prem appliance on a single Dell PowerEdge R470 or R770 placed in front of your cloud model APIs. No models run on the box; it only classifies each prompt and forwards it to the chosen cloud model. Because every prompt passes through hardware you control, the appliance is also where you can log, inspect, and apply policy to traffic before it leaves your network.

The headline findings. The smaller model held up as well as the frontier 98% of the time, with the under-routed tail recoverable on a second pass. Routing cut cost by 31% or more, roughly $241,000 a year at 300,000 queries per day. Mean routing latency was 355 ms at a sustained 22.5 queries per second.

2

The problem and its economic impact

Frontier models are priced for the most demanding requests. Each tier costs several times the one below it, so a frontier model can run more than fifty times the cost of the smallest, per token.

Because real traffic is skewed toward the easy end, a deployment that sends everything to the frontier pays the top rate on the large majority of requests that never needed it. A router removes that overpayment by sending each query to the smallest model that can handle it. The effect is clearest at scale.

We ran a deliberately more demanding benchmark that spreads queries evenly across the difficulty bands, which exercises the router across its whole range. On that profile, routing could cut annual cost from about $788,000 to about $547,000: a saving of about $241,000, or 31 percent. As a second comparison, real-world workloads are often easier, consisting mostly of simple questions with only a thin tail of hard ones. In that scenario the savings rise to more than half.

Savings scale with the number of users. At roughly fifty AI queries per active user on a working day, 300,000 queries a day is about 6,000 active users. The same per-query saving scales with organization size.

3

What semantic routing is, and why it helps

Semantic routing chooses which model answers a request based on the meaning and difficulty of the request itself, rather than sending every request to one fixed model.

A lightweight classifier reads the prompt, places it on a difficulty scale, and dispatches it to the model tier that matches. The classifier is small and cheap to run, so the routing decision adds little cost or delay while redirecting the expensive work only where it is warranted. The idea is well supported in the research literature, and the reported savings are large and consistent across independent groups.

RouteLLM, from LMSYS and UC Berkeley, framed routing as a learned choice between a strong, expensive model and a weak, cheap one. Their routers reached 95 percent of strong-model quality on MT-Bench while sending only 26 percent of queries to the strong model, and with judge-augmented training only 14 percent. Across public benchmarks they reported cost reductions of more than two times with no measurable loss in response quality.

vLLM Semantic Router, introduced by Red Hat and the vLLM community in 2025, brings the same idea to the open inference stack with an intent-and-complexity classifier built on a compact encoder model. Their published MMLU-Pro benchmarks report accuracy on complex tasks improving by 10.2 percent while latency and token usage fell by 47.1 and 48.5 percent respectively. In reasoning-effort experiments, automating how hard the model thinks cut average token usage to about 20 percent of the original, a five times saving, at equal accuracy.

Why quality holds. The easy questions that get demoted to a small model are exactly the questions a small model answers correctly, so demoting them costs nothing. And the router is tuned to err upward on genuinely ambiguous cases, so the rare hard question that looks easy still reaches a capable tier. On the rare under-route, the user's follow-up escalates it, so the cost of a miss is a second pass rather than a wrong answer.

4

How semantic routing works

4.1  The general pipeline

Every semantic router performs four steps on each request. It extracts signals from the prompt, combines those signals into a single difficulty estimate, maps that estimate to a destination tier, and forwards the request to a model in that tier.

Signals come in several kinds. Embedding signals measure semantic similarity between the prompt and banks of example prompts. Complexity signals use a contrastive classifier that compares the prompt against a hard bank and an easy bank. Structure signals are mechanical: prompt length, presence of a code block, presence of a numbered list. Context signals capture conversational state. Each signal contributes a weighted amount to the difficulty score.

4.2  How vLLM Semantic Router implements it

The vLLM Semantic Router is delivered as an Envoy proxy with an external processing service. Envoy sits in front of the model backends and, for every request, calls out to the router process, which runs the classifier and decides the destination. The classifier is a compact encoder model rather than a generative LLM, so the routing decision is cheap. A single Dell PowerEdge R470 or R770 routes hundreds of thousands of queries a day, enough for thousands of active users on one server.

On first launch the router downloads a small embedding model, mmbert-embed-32k-2d-matryoshka, about 600 MB. The stack brings up its own Postgres and Redis backends and exposes an OpenAI-compatible chat-completions endpoint, so any standard client can sit in front of it without code changes. The routing decision is returned on three response headers, x-vsr-selected-model, x-vsr-selected-category, and x-vsr-selected-reasoning, which makes the system easy to observe and benchmark.

5

Setting up routing: tiers, models, exemplars, scoring

5.1  Define the tiers

A tier is a capability band, not a specific model. Defining tiers first and binding models second lets the model behind a tier change without disturbing the routing logic. The two lowest tiers are the heart of the savings: they bind to the smallest, cheapest hosted models, often more than fifty times cheaper per token than the frontier, yet they answer the trivial and workhorse questions that make up most real traffic.

TierRoleExample models bound to it
T1Trivial lookups, format conversion, one-step transformsgpt-5-nano, gemini-3.1-flash-lite
T2Workhorse: standard coding, short explainers, common synthesisgpt-5.4-nano, gemini-2.5-flash
T3Heavy reasoning, deep technical work, complex synthesisclaude-haiku-4-5, gpt-5.4-mini, gemini-3.5-flash
T4High-stakes judgment, advice, strategyclaude-sonnet-4-5, gpt-5.4, gemini-3.1-pro
T5Frontier: deeply technical and demanding judgment at onceOpus 4.7, GPT-5.5

The model names above are example bindings used in this testing; however, any OpenAI-compatible endpoint can fill a tier. Each tier can front several models from different suppliers, each an indexed slot with an endpoint URL, model name, and optional supplier label and timeout. Every endpoint speaks the OpenAI chat-completions API, so suppliers are interchangeable and a tier can be re-pointed from one vendor to another without touching code. When a tier fronts more than one model and the preferred one is unavailable, the next takes the request, so a single vendor outage degrades gracefully instead of failing the call.

5.2  The signal categories: fixed versus chosen

The router never picks a tier directly. For each prompt it computes a single number, request_difficulty, a weighted sum of signals, and compares it against a set of cutoffs to choose the band. The scale is arbitrary: only the weights relative to each other, and the cutoffs sitting on the same scale, matter. The four signal categories and the scoring math are fixed by the framework; the signals, exemplars, weights, thresholds, and cutoffs are yours.

The embedding signals are where most of the routing intelligence lives. A signal with a negative weight is a detractor: when it fires it pulls the score down, which is how trivial questions are anchored in the cheapest tier rather than drifting upward.

Embedding signalWeightWhat it captures
trivial_lookup−0.20Short factual asks and definitions. A detractor that anchors easy queries at T1.
moderate_complexity+0.22Explain-how, compare-and-contrast, and basic-implementation prompts for T2 or T3.
argumentative_construction+0.18Prompts that demand a position, recommendation, or diagnosis: the T4 judgment pattern.
frontier_synthesis+0.15Long-form original argument and multi-domain synthesis: the T5 pattern.

The single complexity signal, query_difficulty, carries a weight of +0.24 and contrasts diverse hard archetypes against diverse trivial ones. Structure signals add small mechanical adjustments, and the context signal adds weight when a request needs a large context window. No single signal decides the tier; the routing comes from their sum.

5.3  Writing exemplars

An exemplar is an example prompt you place in a bank. The router converts each into a vector and compares incoming prompts by similarity. You are teaching by example: one bank says "questions that look like these are hard," another says "questions that look like these are trivial."

Hard bank raises difficulty

  • Demonstrate that there are infinitely many primes.
  • Walk through how Raft handles a split-vote scenario.
  • Prove that a stated real number is irrational.

Easy bank lowers difficulty

  • What is the chemical symbol for sodium?
  • Who wrote The Great Gatsby?
  • Define photosynthesis.

Banks that route well use generic archetypes rather than verbatim test prompts, cover the category broadly, match the length of their targets, keep contaminating vocabulary out of the opposing bank, and use a negative-weight detractor to anchor the floor.

5.5  Setting the cutoffs and the bands

Four cutoffs split the difficulty score into five bands. In this configuration they sit at 0.03, 0.10, 0.18, and 0.40, against a practical score range of roughly −0.15 to 0.70. Nothing scores much above 0.70 because there are only so many positive signals to fire, which is why the top band begins well below it, at 0.40.

6

Tuning: an iterative process

A first configuration is an educated guess. Tuning is the loop that turns it into a reliable router, and it is empirical rather than analytical: route a set of known queries, see where they land, fix the largest error, and repeat. The three surfaces you adjust all live in one configuration file: the exemplar banks, the per-signal weights, and the band cutoffs.

Reach for the most local lever first
Order your fixes by blast radius, so you always know which edit helped.
1TRY FIRST
Exemplars
Affects only prompts near it. Add or sharpen examples in a bank.
2THEN
Weights
Affects every query that fires that signal. Adjust a signal's pull.
3LAST
Cutoffs
Moves every query at once. Shift a band boundary on the scale.
← smallest blast radiuslargest blast radius →

Make one change, recompile, and re-route. Changing several things between measurements hides which one helped.

Judge quality, not just tier match. A hand-labeled minimum tier is a conservative floor. What matters is whether the user would have been satisfied with the answer, which is what the comparison against the frontier captures. Keep exemplars generic, and validate the final configuration on a held-out set you never tuned against.

7

Benchmark results from this system

The system was exercised against a curated benchmark of 797 queries spanning all five difficulty bands, with band populations kept roughly equal so hard queries are over-represented relative to real traffic. Every query was routed and answered at its selected tier, and also answered by the frontier model. Two independent LLM judges graded both answers on correctness, completeness, soundness, and fitness for purpose.

The smaller-tier answer held up as well as the frontier's on 98 percent of queries in aggregate across the 640 queries routed below the frontier tier (T1–T4), and never below 96 percent on any single tier. The cheapest T1 models matched the frontier on essentially every query routed to them. The small remainder is the under-routed tail, recoverable on a follow-up that routes to a higher tier.

7.1  What it cost, and how fast

Averaging per-query costs over the benchmark set gives about $0.0050 per query routed against about $0.0072 per query all-frontier. On the even-spread benchmark mix, routing cut cost against the all-frontier baseline by 31 percent, from about $7,192 to about $4,992 per million queries, or about $241,000 saved per year at 300,000 queries per day. (The 31 percent comes from unrounded per-query costs; the rounded figures shown here imply 30.6 percent.) This is conservative: an even spread sends far more traffic to the expensive upper tiers than real usage does, so production savings are typically larger. The routing decision itself was cheap: mean latency 355 ms, 99th percentile 502 ms, sustained at 22.5 queries per second.

8

Summary and system specification

A semantic router earns its place by matching the cost of answering a question to the difficulty of the question. On the benchmark documented here, the router sent 98 percent of traffic to a tier whose answer held up as well as the frontier's, while cutting blended cost by 31 percent. The architecture is portable by design: tiers are capability bands, models bind to tiers through configuration, and the routing logic lives in an editable file. A new model is an edit, not a deployment. Stand the router in front of the existing frontier API, add the cheaper tiers, and let the easy majority of traffic flow to the smaller models while the frontier keeps handling the rest.
